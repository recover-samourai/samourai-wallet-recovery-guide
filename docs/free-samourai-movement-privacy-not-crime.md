# Free Samourai Movement: Privacy Is Not a Crime

Two years after the April 2024 arrests, the Free Samourai Movement is one of the more durable pieces of privacy advocacy to come out of the post-seizure fallout. This article covers what the movement actually is, what it argues, and how it sits alongside the purely technical recovery work. The primary landing page is [Free Samourai Wallet Movement](https://samourai-wallet.io/free-samourai-wallet-movement).

## Origins

The movement formed in mid-2024, shortly after Rodriguez and Hill were charged. It was never a formal organization - more a loose coalition of former users, privacy advocates, cypherpunks, and a handful of lawyers pushing back on the framing that running a non-custodial CoinJoin coordinator amounted to operating a money transmitting business. It borrowed strategy and rhetoric from earlier campaigns: the Tornado Cash defense, the EFF's long-running work on code-as-speech, and the broader free-software tradition going back to the Bernstein v. US crypto-export cases of the 1990s.

The rallying line - privacy is not a crime - is older than the case. But in 2024 it crystallized around Samourai because the prosecution made a clean test case for the argument: the operators never touched user funds, the CoinJoin protocol was cryptographically auditable, and the coordinator role was narrow enough to be genuinely non-custodial.

## What the movement argues

Three core claims, stripped of rhetoric:

1. Writing and publishing privacy-preserving software is protected activity. The First Amendment treats code as speech when the speech expresses a generalizable technical idea - a position affirmed in Bernstein and subsequent cases.
2. Running a non-custodial coordinator is not money transmission. The operator never takes custody. FinCEN's 2019 guidance distinguished custodial mixers from non-custodial ones, and the Samourai indictment flattened that distinction.
3. Prosecuting privacy tools creates a chilling effect. Developers stop building privacy software not because it is illegal but because the cost of being right in court is ruinous. That is a worse outcome for law enforcement than a working privacy ecosystem.

The [blog article on the Samourai case](https://samourai-wallet.io/blog/privacy-is-not-a-crime-samourai-case) expands those three points with legal references.

## What the movement actually does

- Maintains public pressure through letter-writing campaigns, petitions, and press briefings
- Funds legal defense work for privacy-tool developers
- Keeps recovery documentation alive so former users can access their funds
- Supports the [SAMOURAI token](https://samourai-wallet.io/samourai-token-privacy-coin) as a community-issued symbol associated with the advocacy work

The recovery documentation is the piece most users interact with. The [Samourai Wallet recovery guide](https://samourai-wallet.io/recover-samourai-wallet-funds), [Whirlpool UTXO recovery guide](https://samourai-wallet.io/whirlpool-utxo-recovery-guide), and [Sparrow migration guide](https://samourai-wallet.io/samourai-wallet-to-sparrow-migration) all grew out of this volunteer work.

## The SAMOURAI token

A community-issued token bearing the Samourai name has existed since shortly after the arrests. It is not affiliated with the original Samourai Wallet LLC or with Rodriguez and Hill, and the [token page](https://samourai-wallet.io/samourai-token-privacy-coin) is explicit about this. The token functions as a rallying symbol rather than a utility coin. Do not treat it as financial advice. It exists, it has a community, and that is about the extent of the claim.

## How the movement connects to recovery

If you are reading this because you are trying to get your BTC out of a defunct Samourai Wallet, the movement is largely incidental to your task. What matters for you is the technical flow: install Sparrow or Electrum, import your seed, add the Whirlpool derivation paths. The [30-minute recovery guide](https://samourai-wallet.io/recover-samourai-wallet-funds) covers that and nothing else. The movement's contribution was to keep that documentation alive and in public view, which is not nothing.

If you care about the broader fight - code-as-speech, non-custodial tools, the future of CoinJoin in Western jurisdictions - the [Free Samourai Wallet Movement](https://samourai-wallet.io/free-samourai-wallet-movement) page is the right next stop. If not, recover your coins and move on. Both are legitimate responses.

## Beyond Samourai

The case set a precedent that influenced how every subsequent privacy-tool project approaches legal structure. Wasabi restructured around a different operator entity. New CoinJoin coordinators launch from clearer jurisdictions. The [Wasabi Wallet review](https://samourai-wallet.io/wasabi-wallet-review) touches on how the current CoinJoin landscape looks in 2026. The broader privacy ecosystem shifted in response.

For acquisition, the [no-KYC Bitcoin wallet](https://samourai-wallet.io/no-kyc-bitcoin-wallet) guide covers the onramp side of the privacy stack, which matters because using a fully private wallet with KYC-tagged coins defeats most of the work.

## The bigger picture

The argument the movement makes is narrow on purpose. It is not "mixers should be legal regardless of use." It is "a non-custodial mixer is not money transmission, and prosecuting one under a money-transmission statute bends the law out of shape." That is a defensible position and one you do not have to agree with in full to think the case raises genuine First Amendment concerns.

Whether the movement changes any policy outcomes in the next few years is an open question. Whether it is currently keeping recovery documentation alive for tens of thousands of former Samourai users is not - that is happening, and the repository you are reading is part of it.
